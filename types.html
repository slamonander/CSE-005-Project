types of malware.

(data systems) (information theft) (nuisance)

data systems and disruptors.

Malwares designed to damage, encrypt, or steal data, disrupt a computer's system, and demand ransom while holding the user's data hostage.

1. ransomware

A type of malware that disables the user's access to their data or device through encryption until the user pays a ransom to the attacker. The earliest types of ransomware attacks only involved demanding a ransom for the encryption key that is needed to regain access to the user's data or device.

However, recent ransomware attacks now include double or triple extortion.

Double extortion attacks: Steals the user's data and leaks it online.

Triple extortion attacks: Threats to use stolen data to attack the user's connections.

2. wiper malware

A malware used to erase the user's data and ensure the data cannot be recovered. Wiper malware destroys the data through encrypting/overwriting the files' contents or attack the operating system itself.

These malwares are used to take down computer networks in companies and organizations as well as cover up traces left after an intrusion.

Unfortunately, these types of malwares have been increasingly used for cyberwarfare. An example of this malware used during wartime is the HermeticWiper, used in the Russian invasian against Ukraine.

3. viruses

A common type of malware that aims to steal data, launch DDoS attacks, or damage the user's device or network. This can result to disrupted operations, hijacked systems, and corrupted, altered, or deleted data.

Viruses can replicate themselves in order to spread onto other devices and networks. The infection and the spreading is possible through malicious downloads and attachments and they are mainly attached to an executable file (.exe).

These viruses do not activate unless the user opens or runs the infected program.

4. worms

A self-replicating malware where the only difference from a virus is that it can replicate without "activation" from the user.

Normally, these malwares access the system through backdoors built into the software, software vulnerabilities, flash drives, phishing, or networks.

Worms attack by:

  • Consuming bandwidth
  • Deleting files
  • Stealing data
  • Overloading networks
  • Dropping other types of malwares into the system

information theft.

Malwares used to capture and monitor the user's activities, steal data, and compromise systems on devices.

1. spyware

A malware that invades a user's device to steal and collect the user's sensitive information, track login credentials and location, and monitor the user's data activity. The goals behind these attacks are usually to steal credit card numbers, banking information, and passwords.

Spywares are often installed through malicious downloads, browsing on a malicious website, or as an app install package.

Spywares are hard to detect, but the signs of an active spyware is if it reduces the processor, network connection speeds, data usage, and battery life.

2. keyloggers

A type of spyware that logs and monitors your keyboard strokes, which allows the attackers to know and record what you are typing. This includes passwords, banking information, what pages you have visited, etc.

Keyloggers are usually installed into a system through phishing, social engineering, or malicious downloads and softwares.

3. trojans

A type of malware made to damage, disrupt, steal, and hijack the user's system, data, or network.

The reason why trojans have their name is because it can disguise itself as a legitimate code, deceiving the user and avoid getting flagged by the system easily. However, trojans has to be "activated" by the user and it cannot replicate itself.

Other attacks by trojans include:

  • DDoS attacks
  • Installing malicious programs and malwares
  • Steal account information and data
  • Allow remote access to your device

4. mobile malware

A type of malware that targets mobile devices , including tablets and smartphones. Jailbroken phones are especially vulnerable to these attacks due to the lack of protection from the device's original operating system.

These malwares can be distributed through phishing and malicious downloads.

Mobile malware attacks include:

  • Stealing financial information and passwords
  • Conduct cryptomining on a victim's device
  • Hijack a device to generate income through fake ad clicks

nuisance and advertising.

Malwares that bombards users with unwanted advertisements, compromise systems, and exploit users.

1. adware

A malware known to generate revenue by tracking a user's activity to determine what advertisements to generate and send to the user. This also means that the user's activity is collected to create a profile of the user to share or sell to advertisers without the user's knowledge or consent.

Adware pops up as unwanted pop-up advertisements on your device. They can be installed through malicious programs, downloads, or a vulnerability in the software or operating system.

2. fileless malware

A software that uses legitimate programs to evade the antiviruses and cyber security defenses. It does not rely on files and does not leave traces of its intrusion on the system. Fileless malwares are installed through phishing and social engineering and can attack by:

  • Changing files that are native to the operating system
  • Gain access to the system
  • Steal credentials
  • Collect the user's data
  • Set up a backdoor for attackers to access the system more easily

3. bots

A malware that are automated, self-replicating, and come from malicious downloads and phishing. It performs pre-defined tasks and imitates user behavior.

A network of bots, called a botnet, is a network of devices that is running 1 or more bots. This means that it makes botnet traffic come from multiple IP addresses. Bots can launch a broad flood of attacks, ranging from DDoS attacks to hacking user accounts or devices.

4. rootkits

A software that can remotely control a user's computer with full administrator privileges while hiding itself from the system. They can be installed into applications, kernels, hypervisors, or firmware through phishing, malicious downloads, and compromised share drives.

The capabilities rootkits have are:

  • Remotely execute files
  • Remote access to the system
  • Log files
  • Spy on user without user's knowledge
  • Create backdoors in the system
  • Steal user's data
